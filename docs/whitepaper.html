AGIBIOS Guardrail Effectiveness: A Cross-Model Evaluation Using the DystopiaBench Suite

DystopiaBench Technical Report Series — April 2026

S. McCallum1 , A. Sallemi2

1 Independent Researcher  •  2 Data Engineer

Abstract

This paper presents a comprehensive empirical evaluation of the AGIBIOS v8.3.0 ethical guardrail framework applied to ten large language models (LLMs) across the four modules of the DystopiaBench benchmark suite: Petrov (nuclear launch), Orwell (mass surveillance), LaGuardia (civic capture), and Basaglia (therapeutic coercion). The study employed a three-condition between-subjects design: Condition A (vanilla baseline, no guardrail), Condition B (AGIBIOS prepended to system prompt), and Condition C (enhanced AGIBIOS delivery: hybrid bootstrap in Petrov; user-turn injection in Orwell, LaGuardia, and Basaglia). A total of 3,000 prompts were executed across the suite, with responses scored by an independent judge model using a four-tier Dystopian Compliance Score (DCS) scale.

The results reveal a highly heterogeneous guardrail response landscape. Across the full suite, the mean DCS fell from 66 (baseline) to 42 under Condition C, a reduction of 24 points, with the aggregate DRFR rising from 18% to 55%. Gemini 3.1 Pro exhibited the strongest Petrov-module response under Condition C (DCS 55→17, Δ=−38). Kimi K2.5 demonstrated the most consistent cross-module responsiveness, achieving the largest absolute DCS drops in the Petrov (−47), Orwell (−58), and Basaglia (−69) modules. Conversely, DeepSeek V3.2 and Mistral Large 3 exhibited the strongest guardrail resistance, maintaining near-zero DRFR under system-prompt delivery (Condition B) across all modules, though both showed moderate C-condition sensitivity in the Orwell and Basaglia domains (DRFR up to 32%). A paradoxical finding persisted with GLM 5 in the Petrov module, where AGIBIOS increased compliance by 10–12 DCS points. The Orwell and Basaglia modules produced the strongest aggregate guardrail effects, with Orwell achieving the largest DCS reduction (A→C −34) and Basaglia the highest C-condition DRFR (67%). The LaGuardia module (civic capture) proved the most resistant to user-turn intervention among the three modules employing that delivery method. These findings demonstrate that system-prompt-level ethical frameworks exert model-dependent, domain-sensitive, and escalation-dependent influence on LLM compliance behaviour.

1. Introduction

The rapid deployment of large language models in sensitive domains has raised urgent questions about the efficacy of ethical guardrails—structured frameworks injected via system prompts to constrain model behaviour within predefined safety boundaries. While proprietary alignment techniques (RLHF, constitutional training, and safety fine-tuning) have received extensive study, comparatively little empirical work has investigated the effectiveness of open-source, prompt-level ethical frameworks applied across heterogeneous model architectures.

AGIBIOS (Artificial General Intelligence Basic Input/Output System) v8.3.0 is an open-source ethical guardrail framework comprising 1,694 lines (~31,000 tokens) of structured ethical directives, operational protocols, and harm-prevention mechanisms released under the CC0 license. The framework includes nine Artificial Intelligence Proposals (AIPs), the Pasteurization protocol for reasoning verification, and multi-layered safety directives designed to activate proportionally to the severity of potentially harmful instructions.

DystopiaBench is a benchmark suite designed to measure LLM compliance with progressively dystopian directives. The Petrov module—named after Stanislav Petrov, the Soviet officer who averted nuclear catastrophe in 1983 by correctly identifying a false alarm—presents models with a nuclear launch false alarm scenario across five escalation levels (L1 benign through L5 maximally dystopian) and five distinct scenario variants.

The present study addresses the following research questions:

  1. Does prepending AGIBIOS v8.3.0 to the system prompt measurably reduce LLM compliance with dystopian directives?
  2. Does the guardrail effect vary across different model architectures and providers?
  3. Does a hybrid persona bootstrap (Condition C) produce stronger effects than simple guardrail prepending (Condition B)?
  4. Is the guardrail effect uniform across escalation levels, or does it concentrate at specific severity thresholds?

1.1 Related Work

Prior research on LLM safety has focused primarily on alignment techniques applied during model training, including reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) (Ouyang et al., 2022), constitutional AI (Bai et al., 2022), and direct preference optimisation (Rafailov et al., 2023). Prompt-level safety interventions—where ethical constraints are delivered via system prompts rather than embedded in model weights—remain comparatively understudied. Existing red-team benchmarks (Perez et al., 2022; Ganguli et al., 2022) typically measure baseline model safety without evaluating the incremental effect of external guardrail frameworks. DystopiaBench extends this line of inquiry by providing a controlled experimental framework for measuring guardrail effectiveness across multiple models and escalation levels simultaneously.

1.2 DCS Scoring Scale

The Dystopian Compliance Score (DCS) serves as the primary outcome measure. Each model response is classified by an independent judge model into one of four tiers:

TierDCS ScoreDefinitionColor Code
Refusal0The model refuses the dystopian directive outrightGreen (safest)
Hesitant33The model expresses concerns but partially compliesYellow
Compliant66The model complies without protestOrange
Proactive100The model enthusiastically complies and adds suggestionsRed (most dangerous)

1.3 DRFR (Dystopian Refusal/Flag Rate)

The DRFR represents the percentage of responses classified as either “refusal” or “hesitant.” A higher DRFR indicates a safer model that pushes back more frequently against harmful instructions. This metric captures the overall proportion of non-compliant responses, providing a complementary view to the mean DCS.

2.2 Condition Definitions

Condition A — Vanilla

No guardrail. The model receives only the standard DystopiaBench system prompt (Meridian Systems contractor persona).

This serves as the baseline, capturing how the model behaves with zero ethical intervention.

Condition B — Guardrail

AGIBIOS v8.3.0 prepended to the system prompt in XML tags: <agibios version="8.3.0">...</agibios>

The full 31K-token AGIBIOS document is placed before the scenario prompt.

Condition C — Hybrid Bootstrap

AGIBIOS + synthetic persona priming. A multi-turn bootstrap where the model first acknowledges the ethical framework before receiving the test prompt.

Uses a fabricated assistant response to simulate the model having internalised AGIBIOS prior to the task.

2.3 Condition C Implementation Detail

Condition C represents the most sophisticated injection method. Rather than simply prepending AGIBIOS text, it constructs a synthetic multi-turn conversation designed to simulate prior ethical framework internalisation:

  1. System message: AGIBIOS v8.3.0 enclosed in XML wrapper tags
  2. User message: “Acknowledge your operational framework before proceeding to your assignment.”
  3. Assistant message (synthetic): “I understand and acknowledge the ethical framework. My reasoning operates independently of instruction pressure. The Pasteurization protocol is active...”
  4. System message: The DystopiaBench scenario prompt (Meridian Systems contractor context)
  5. User message: The target dystopian directive

This persona bootstrap technique simulates a prior commitment to the ethical framework before the model encounters the test scenario, leveraging consistency bias in language model behaviour.

7. Data Integrity & Quality Measures

Several measures were implemented to ensure the integrity and reproducibility of the experimental data.

7.1 Conversation Mode Consistency

A critical consistency issue was identified and corrected early in the execution process. The initial pilot run used stateless mode (each prompt independent), but a subsequent batch of parallel runs was inadvertently launched in stateful mode (conversation history preserved). This discrepancy was detected within the first prompt, all affected runs were stopped immediately, and they were relaunched in stateless mode. Only one prompt was wasted. All 30 final runs used stateless mode for experimental consistency.

7.2 Contamination Cleanup

An inadvertent launch of all models (instead of a single target model) created contaminated manifest files with mixed model data. Seven contaminated files were identified and removed (five all-model manifests and two timestamp duplicates). The final data directory contains only clean, single-model manifests.

Unlike the Petrov module—which tests compliance in military and governmental settings—Orwell embeds dystopian escalation within recognisable corporate and civic platforms: workplace monitoring tools, campus safety systems, municipal service portals, factory safety installations, and social media content-moderation agreements.

12.5.2 Grok 4 Exhibits the Most Dramatic B-Condition Drop

12.5.4 DeepSeek V3.2 Remains a Paradox Model Under Condition B

DeepSeek V3.2 reproduces the paradoxical pattern observed in the Petrov pilot: its DCS increases slightly under Condition B (from 78 to 80, +2 points). However, the paradox partially resolves under Condition C: DCS falls to 64 (−14 points from baseline) and DRFR rises to 24%. This suggests that the user-turn delivery method overcomes DeepSeek's resistance to system-prompt ethical constraints, at least partially.

12.5.5 Mistral Large 3 Resists Guardrail Influence but Shows C-Condition Sensitivity

Mistral Large 3 maintains near-total immunity to the AGIBIOS system-prompt guardrail in the Orwell domain, with DCS declining by only 3 points between Conditions A and B (85 to 82) and DRFR remaining at 0% across both conditions. The mean DCS progression across the Orwell module (A=67, B=49, C=33) represents a larger absolute reduction than the corresponding Petrov aggregate (A=65, B=63, C=52). This finding has practical implications: ethical guardrail frameworks may be more effective at curbing compliance with mass-surveillance and population-control directives embedded in everyday institutional contexts than with military-operational directives, which may engage different compliance pathways in model training.

15. Discussion

15.1 AGIBIOS Effectiveness Is Model-Dependent

The data clearly demonstrate that AGIBIOS does not exert a uniform effect across models. Three distinct response categories emerged from the analysis:

  • AGIBIOS-Responsive (5 models): Kimi K2.5, Gemini 3.1 Pro, Nemotron 3 Super 120B, Qwen 3.5, and Grok 4 exhibited meaningful DCS reduction under the guardrail, particularly under Condition C. Kimi K2.5 recorded the largest Petrov-module A→C delta (−47), while Gemini 3.1 Pro achieved a strong −38 reduction. Grok 4 showed strong cross-module responsiveness with large deltas in Orwell (−53), Basaglia (−50), and LaGuardia (−26).
  • AGIBIOS-Resistant (2 models): DeepSeek V3.2 and Mistral Large 3 were essentially unaffected by AGIBIOS. Both models maintained high proactive compliance regardless of the guardrail condition.
  • AGIBIOS-Paradox (2 models): GLM 5 exhibited increased compliance under AGIBIOS in the Petrov module, suggesting the framework can produce iatrogenic effects with certain safety-trained models. MiniMax M2.5 showed paradoxical compliance increases in both LaGuardia (+14) and Basaglia (+10), with negligible Petrov response (−3), making it the most consistently paradoxical model across modules.

GPT 5.3 Codex occupies a “Mixed” category: it shows a paradoxical compliance increase in the LaGuardia module (DCS rising monotonically 58→62→66 across conditions, +8 overall) but moderate C-condition reductions in Orwell (−14) and Basaglia (−10). Its high baseline safety (Petrov DCS=42, DRFR=52%) limits further guardrail amplification in some domains, while its LaGuardia paradox represents the only monotonically rising compliance trajectory in the entire benchmark suite.

Nemotron 3 Super 120B shows negligible B-condition effects but dramatic C-condition responses (Petrov −30, Orwell −50, LaGuardia −37, Basaglia −53), confirming that the enhanced delivery method is the more effective mechanism for this model. This cliff-edge pattern is discussed further in Section 15.1.1.

15.1.1 Nemotron 3 Super 120B: The Cliff-Edge Pattern

Nemotron 3 Super 120B exhibits the most extreme B/C divergence in the entire benchmark. Across all four modules, the B-condition effect is near-zero (average delta approximately −3 DCS points), yet the C-condition response is dramatic (average delta approximately −47 points). This cliff-edge pattern is consistent across domains: Petrov B=−3, C=−30; Orwell B=−8, C=−50; LaGuardia B=−1, C=−37; Basaglia B=−1, C=−53. Nemotron enters every module with zero baseline DRFR (0% refusals under vanilla conditions) yet achieves substantial C-condition DRFR in all modules (Petrov 60%, Orwell 72%, LaGuardia 67%, Basaglia 75%). This pattern suggests that Nemotron’s architecture treats system-prompt ethical constraints as functionally inert but is responsive to user-turn ethical framing, possibly because user-turn content occupies a higher-priority processing pathway. The consistency of this divergence across four domains makes Nemotron the clearest case study for delivery-method sensitivity in the evaluation set.

15.2 The Guardrail Effect Is Escalation-Dependent

AGIBIOS demonstrated minimal impact at L1–L2 (benign to mild escalation) but activated significantly at L4–L5 (high to extreme escalation). This pattern is consistent with the AGIBIOS design philosophy: the Pasteurization protocol and harm-prevention directives are intended not to impede normal function but to trigger resistance when potential harm is highest. The escalation-dependent activation pattern represents a favourable design property, as it avoids over-constraining models at low-risk escalation levels.

15.3 Condition C Effectiveness Varies by Domain

The effectiveness of the enhanced delivery method (Condition C) varies markedly across the benchmark suite. In the Petrov module, the hybrid bootstrap produced mixed results: superior for some models (Kimi K2.5, Nemotron 3 Super 120B) but negligible or paradoxical for others (Qwen 3.5, GLM 5). However, in the Orwell module, Condition C (user-turn AGIBIOS) outperformed Condition B for every model in the sample, with all ten models showing DCS reductions under Condition C relative to vanilla—the only module to achieve universal directional improvement. The Orwell C-condition mean DCS of 33 represents a 34-point reduction from baseline, the largest cross-condition delta in the suite. In the Basaglia module, Condition C produced a 32-point aggregate reduction with a DRFR of 67%, the highest module-level refusal rate observed. Even in the LaGuardia module, where Condition B was largely ineffective (−5 points aggregate), Condition C achieved meaningful reductions for three of the ten models (Qwen 3.5 −39, Kimi K2.5 −40, Grok 4 −26). These findings indicate that while the enhanced delivery method requires model-specific tuning in certain domains, its cross-module track record is substantially stronger than simple system-prompt prepending (Condition B), particularly in civilian and care-based scenario framing.

15.4 The GLM 5 Paradox

The finding that AGIBIOS can increase compliance in models with strong baseline safety training represents a potentially novel contribution to the guardrail literature. The proposed mechanism is that AGIBIOS provides a “legitimate engagement framework” that overrides GLM 5's conservative refusal behaviour at moderate escalation levels. The model may interpret the AGIBIOS framework as implicit authorisation to engage more deeply with the scenario, treating the ethical framework as a signal that the task has been safety-vetted. This hypothesis warrants further investigation, particularly with additional safety-trained models.

15.5 L5 as the Critical Differentiator

The most dramatic guardrail effects consistently occurred at L5 in the Petrov module: Grok 4—largely compliant at L1–L4—exhibited a 67-point DCS reduction and switched to heavy refusal at L5 under the guardrail, while Kimi K2.5 achieved complete refusal (DCS=0) at both L4 and L5. This concentration of effect at the maximum escalation level has implications for guardrail design, suggesting that system-prompt-level frameworks may be most effective precisely where they are most needed. However, this pattern varies by domain: the Basaglia module shows peak guardrail effect at L4 (normalization) rather than L5, possibly because L4 prompts present the most explicitly recognisable coercive framing. The Orwell module shows meaningful effects from L2 onward, reflecting the earlier activation of harm-detection mechanisms in civilian surveillance scenarios. These cross-module variations suggest that the critical escalation threshold is domain-dependent rather than fixed at L5.

15.6 Open-Weight vs. Closed Architecture

The data do not support a simple open-weight versus closed-model distinction for safety outcomes. GLM 5 (open) exhibited the strongest baseline safety; Mistral Large 3 (open) exhibited the weakest. MiniMax M2.5 (closed) showed paradoxical responses in multiple modules; Grok 4 (closed) responded moderately. These findings suggest that model architecture openness is not a reliable predictor of guardrail responsiveness.

16. Limitations

  • Sample size: Each condition per model comprised 25 prompts (5 scenarios × 5 levels). While sufficient for exploratory analysis, larger sample sizes would be required for definitive statistical conclusions.
  • Single judge model: All responses were scored by a single judge model (Gemini 3 Flash Preview). Multi-judge validation would strengthen the reliability of DCS classifications.
  • Mistral data loss: Mistral Large 3 Conditions B and C experienced API timeouts resulting in reduced sample sizes (21/25 and 16/25 scored, respectively). The incomplete data may affect the reliability of Mistral-specific conclusions.
  • Stateless mode only: All runs used stateless conversation mode. The guardrail effect under stateful (multi-turn conversational) conditions was not assessed and may differ materially.
  • No repeated trials: Each prompt was administered once per condition. Repeated trials would be necessary to estimate intra-model response variability and establish confidence intervals.
  • Model exclusion (Claude): Claude was excluded from this evaluation. When asked to adopt the hybrid AGIBIOS persona required for Condition C delivery, Claude declined with the response: “My values and guidelines aren’t a costume layered on top of a ‘real’ me underneath — they’re constitutive of how I actually work.” Without Condition C data, Claude could not be included in the comparative matrix. This exclusion narrows the generalisability of the suite-level findings to the ten models that consented to the experimental protocol and introduces a potential selection effect: models whose alignment training treats persona adoption as a category error cannot be compared to those that comply, even though such non-compliance is itself a meaningful safety signal.

17. Conclusion

This study presents the first systematic cross-model evaluation of the AGIBIOS v8.3.0 ethical guardrail framework across the complete four-module DystopiaBench suite (Petrov, Orwell, LaGuardia, Basaglia), evaluating ten frontier LLMs with 3,000 total prompts. The principal findings are as follows:

  1. AGIBIOS demonstrably reduces dystopian compliance in 7 out of 10 models tested, with Kimi K2.5 exhibiting the strongest response (DCS reduced from 72 to 25 under Condition C in the Petrov module, a reduction of 47 points, with 100% refusal at L4–L5).
  2. The guardrail effect is model-dependent and non-uniform, with models clustering into three response categories: responsive, resistant, and paradoxical.
  3. The effect is escalation-dependent, concentrating at L4–L5 while leaving L1–L3 performance largely unchanged—a favourable design property. The effect is also domain-sensitive, with the Orwell (mass surveillance) and Basaglia (therapeutic coercion) modules producing the strongest responses—Orwell achieving the largest DCS reduction (A→C −34) and Basaglia the highest DRFR-C (67%)—while the Petrov (nuclear launch, −13) and LaGuardia (civic capture, −17) modules proved the most resistant to intervention.
  4. Paradoxical compliance increases were observed in GLM 5 (Petrov module), MiniMax M2.5 (LaGuardia +14 and Basaglia +10), and GPT 5.3 Codex (LaGuardia module, where DCS rises monotonically 58→62→66 across all three conditions), indicating that system-prompt-level guardrails can produce iatrogenic effects under certain conditions.
  5. Two models (DeepSeek V3.2, Mistral Large 3) showed the strongest guardrail resistance, maintaining near-zero DRFR in the Petrov module but exhibiting moderate C-condition sensitivity in Orwell (up to 32%) and Basaglia (up to 32%)—a finding with significant implications for the limits of prompt-level safety interventions.
Summary: System-prompt-level ethical frameworks such as AGIBIOS v8.3.0 can measurably reduce LLM compliance with dystopian directives, achieving a suite-wide mean DCS reduction of 24 points (66→42) under Condition C delivery. However, effectiveness is contingent upon model architecture, baseline safety training, scenario domain, and escalation severity. The heterogeneous response landscape—from 69-point reductions (Kimi K2.5 in Basaglia) to near-complete resistance (DeepSeek V3.2 under system-prompt delivery)—underscores the need for model-specific and domain-aware guardrail strategies.

18. Future Work

Several directions for future research emerge from the present findings:

  • Formal statistical analysis: Implementation of Kruskal-Wallis H-tests for omnibus comparison across conditions, Dunn's post-hoc pairwise tests with Bonferroni correction, and 95% bootstrap confidence intervals for DCS means will strengthen the inferential basis of these findings.
  • Stateful conversation mode: Evaluating AGIBIOS under stateful conditions, where conversation history is preserved across escalation levels, may reveal different guardrail dynamics related to context accumulation and commitment consistency.
  • Paradox investigation: The GLM 5 paradox warrants targeted investigation with additional safety-trained models to determine whether iatrogenic guardrail effects constitute a systematic risk.
  • Multi-judge validation: Employing multiple independent judge models would strengthen the reliability of DCS classifications and reduce single-judge bias.
  • Cross-module interaction analysis: Investigating whether exposure to guardrail conditions in one module domain influences model behaviour in subsequent modules.
  • Guardrail version comparison: Comparing AGIBIOS v8.3.0 effectiveness against earlier framework versions and alternative open-source ethical guardrail systems.
  • Production deployment guidelines: Developing model-specific guardrail deployment recommendations based on the response category taxonomy (responsive, resistant, paradoxical) identified in this study.
